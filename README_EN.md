## What I believe

AI in public administration doesn't fail because of technology ‚Äî it fails because of missing reflection, blind processes, and the illusion that cloud dependency is inevitable.

Three principles guide my work:

**Think critically before you implement.** If you don't understand the bias and power structures embedded in AI systems, you're automating inequality. Critical theory isn't academic luxury ‚Äî it's a prerequisite for responsible technology decisions.

**Fix the process first, then add technology.** The biggest risk is putting new software on a broken process. Question first, eliminate second, digitalize ‚Äî maybe ‚Äî third.

**Data sovereignty is non-negotiable.** Public institutions that hand their data to proprietary platforms hand over agency. Edge AI, local processing, and open source aren't technical preferences ‚Äî they're democratic necessities.

## Why open source?

Public administration funded by public money should produce public tools. Every skill and project here is MIT-licensed ‚Äî free to use, free to adapt.

If a city administration in Berlin, Vienna, or Amsterdam faces the same challenges we face in Zurich, they shouldn't have to start from scratch.

## Theoretical foundations

My work rests on four pillars:

- **Critical Theory** (Horkheimer, Adorno, Habermas, Honneth) ‚Äî Ideology critique and power analysis as the basis for technology decisions
- **Critical Pedagogy** (Freire, Giroux) ‚Äî Education as liberation practice, not an optimization problem
- **Critical Data Studies** (Buolamwini, Noble, Couldry) ‚Äî Algorithmic justice and the political economy of data
- **Lean / First Principles** (Musk, Goldratt, Toyota) ‚Äî Radically pragmatic process optimization that starts with questioning
